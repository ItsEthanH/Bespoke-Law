The Benefits of Creating a Will at All Ages of Adulthood

Do Young Adults Need to Think About Wills?

It might seem premature to think about a will in your twenties or thirties, but life is unpredictable. A will can dictate how to handle your financial assets, digital accounts, and personal belongings. It can also specify who will look after your pets or any debts you may leave behind, ensuring nothing is left to chance. While it isn’t talked about as often as it should be, a will is simply part of responsible adulthood, especially when you have a spouse or other close family in the picture.

Wills For Parents

If you have children, having a will is non-negotiable. It not only covers who inherits your assets, but can also appoint a guardian for your children. This decision alone can't be left to assumptions. You need every aspect of your estate to be legally documented if you want your wishes to take effect in the unlikely event that a tragedy occurs, or your become incapacitated.

Creating a Will As You Enter Your Golden Years

As you accumulate more assets, think about minimizing inheritance taxes, and begin to wonder about the legacy you’ll leave behind, a will can help manage these complexities and help you sleep at night. Ideally you will already have a will in place, but It’s also a time to consider updating your legal documents to reflect any changes in your life circumstances, such as the arrival of grandchildren or the loss of a loved one.

Different Types of Wills to Choose From

Attorney For Simple Wills in Myrtle Beach

In South Carolina, a simple will is a straightforward way to ensure that your assets are distributed according to your wishes. This type of will is perfect for those with uncomplicated estates and clear ideas about where their property should go. Under South Carolina law, your simple will must be in writing, signed by you, and witnessed by two individuals who are present at the same time. These witnesses must not be beneficiaries to avoid conflicts of interest.

Testamentary Trust Wills

These are for those who wish to establish a trust upon their death to manage certain assets for the beneficiaries. This type of will is ideal for managing inheritances for minors or family members who may need additional oversight. Under SC law, the testamentary trust doesn’t come into effect until after your death and after the will has gone through probate. Our will formation attorneys can help you decide if this type of will is a good fit for your estate. If you need help from a trust fund lawyer in Myrtle Beach, please give us a call.

Joint Wills

Often made by married couples, a joint will is a single document that covers both individuals, typically stating that when one dies, the entirety of the estate goes to the survivor. Once one spouse passes away, the joint will becomes irrevocable, preventing the surviving spouse from making any changes to the agreed terms. This does provide a great deal of security, but it also requires careful consideration because of the permanency of this kind of will.

Advance Directives in SC

Advance directives are instructions regarding your healthcare if you become unable to make decisions for yourself. These include living wills and medical powers of attorney. A living will in SC allows you to outline your wishes regarding life-sustaining treatment if you're terminally ill or permanently unconscious. A medical power of attorney, on the other hand, designates someone you trust to make healthcare decisions on your behalf.

How to Make a Will Legally Binding in South Carolina

In South Carolina, making a will legally binding isn't complicated, but it must be done right. Here’s what you need to ensure:

  • Age and Capacity: Are you 18 years of age or older and in sound mind?
  • Voluntarily Made: Was the will made without coercion?
  • In Writing: Oral wills are not recognized in the state of South Carolina
  • Signatures: You must sign the will in the presence of two witnesses (these witnesses must also sign the will)

Other Considerations Regarding Your Will in Myrtle Beach

Get Your Will Notarized

While notarization is not a legal requirement for the validity of a will in South Carolina, opting to have your will notarized can be beneficial. A notarized will is considered 'self-proving,' which means the court can accept the will without requiring the testimony of the witnesses who signed it. This is a good idea for those who wish to leave nothing to chance, or if you have an inkling that the will may be contested.

Updating Your Will

Don’t forget to revisit your will with a qualified estate planning attorney from time to time. More children, deaths of loved ones, divorces and marriages, and other life changes may necessitate revisions.

Intestacy Laws in South Carolina

If you die without a will in South Carolina, your assets will be distributed according to state intestacy laws, which might not align with your personal wishes. These laws prioritize your closest relatives, with your spouse and children typically first in line. If you are unmarried with no children, assets go to other family members like parents, siblings, or more distant relatives. According to section Section 62-2-105, estates can even pass to the state of South Carolina if there are no takers.
