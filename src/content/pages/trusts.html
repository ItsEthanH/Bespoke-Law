What is a Trust?

When you set up a trust, you're essentially putting a trusted person or institution in charge of managing your assets for the benefit of someone else—your beneficiary. There are two main types of trusts to consider: revocable and irrevocable. With a revocable trust, you keep control over the assets and can make changes or even dissolve the trust if your situation changes. It’s like keeping the keys to a safety deposit box. On the other hand, an irrevocable trust is more like giving the keys away; it offers stronger protection for your assets, but locks in the terms permanently.

Benefits of Establishing a Trust

Think of a trust as a tool that can streamline and secure your estate’s future. One of its biggest perks is skipping the probate process, which can often be lengthy and public, by directly passing assets to your beneficiaries. Trusts also defend your estate from creditors and legal claims, keeping your hard-earned assets safe. Trusts also provide tax benefits, as they can shave off significant amounts from what would otherwise go to taxes. Plus, you get to call the shots on how and when your assets are distributed, all while keeping these details private from prying eyes.

Types of Trusts Commonly Used in Myrtle Beach, SC

Each type of trust serves a unique purpose. Revocable Living Trusts are great for keeping control over your assets while you're alive, and then passing them directly to your beneficiaries without the hassle of probate. Irrevocable Trusts, on the other hand, offer solid protection for your assets from lawsuits or creditors.

For families with a disabled child or relative, Special Needs Trusts ensure they’re taken care of without risking their eligibility for public benefits. Charitable Trusts, another popular vehicle, can be a meaningful way to contribute to causes you care about while enjoying tax breaks. And for those planning for young ones, Trusts for Minors ensure your children or grandchildren are financially sound as they step into adulthood.

Trust-Related Laws in South Carolina

Navigating trust laws can be tricky, but here’s what you need to know about setting up trusts in South Carolina. Our laws, detailed in the South Carolina Trust Code, provide a solid framework for creating valid trusts. For instance, the law requires clear beneficiaries and a proper setup by the trust's creator. For those who like flexibility, South Carolina law typically allows you to amend or revoke revocable trusts unless explicitly stated otherwise. Also, trustees are required to manage your trust with a high standard of care—they are considered a fiduciary. In SC, trusts are not administered by Probate Court, placing more responsibility on the trust administrator.
